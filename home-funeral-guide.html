Home Funerals: A Family-Directed Alternative

A home funeral is an act of profound love. It is the decision by a family to care for their own dead — to wash, dress, and watch over the body of someone they love in the familiar setting of home, surrounded by the people and the places that mattered most. For most of human history, this was not a choice; it was simply what families did. Today, a growing number of families are returning to this deeply personal tradition, and discovering that caring for a loved one after death can be one of the most healing and meaningful experiences of their lives. This guide is here to walk you through every aspect of home funerals with honesty, compassion, and practical detail, so you can make informed decisions that feel right for your family. Whatever path you choose, it is the right one if it comes from a place of love.

What Is a Home Funeral?

A home funeral is an after-death care arrangement in which the family takes an active, hands-on role in caring for the body of a deceased loved one, holding a vigil or ceremony, and coordinating the practical and legal steps that follow a death — all from the comfort and familiarity of home. Instead of calling a funeral home immediately after death and transferring the body into professional care, the family keeps the person at home, typically for one to three days, and provides the care themselves.

In a home funeral, family members may bathe and dress the body, apply cooling to preserve it naturally, set up a room for visitation and vigil, file the necessary paperwork, and eventually transport the body to a crematory or cemetery — or arrange for those services as needed. Some families handle every step independently. Others work with a home funeral guide, death midwife, or sympathetic funeral director who provides support and expertise while allowing the family to remain in charge.

The defining feature of a home funeral is not where it happens, but who directs it. In a home funeral, the family is not a passive consumer of services. The family is the caregiver, the decision-maker, and the ceremony-maker. This shift — from purchasing a package of services to actively caring for someone you love — is what makes home funerals so transformative for the families who choose them.

Interest in home funerals has grown steadily over the past two decades. According to the National Home Funeral Alliance, inquiries from families have increased significantly year after year. The reasons are as varied as the families themselves: a desire for intimacy and control during a vulnerable time, a wish to honor cultural or spiritual traditions, concern about the environmental impact of conventional funerals, the need to reduce costs, or simply the feeling that caring for one's own is the most natural and loving thing to do.

History and Context

For the vast majority of human history, families cared for their own dead. There were no funeral homes, no embalming chemicals, no metal caskets, and no industry built around death. When a person died, their family washed and dressed the body, laid them out in the parlor or the main room of the house, and invited neighbors and community members to come and pay their respects. The body was kept cool with ice or simply by the temperature of the room. After a period of vigil — usually one to three days — the family buried the body on their property, in a churchyard, or in a community cemetery. The wooden coffin, if one was used, was often built by a family member or a local carpenter.

This practice was universal across cultures. In America, it was the standard way of handling death well into the late 19th century. The shift toward professionalized funeral care began during the Civil War, when the need to transport soldiers' bodies long distances for burial led to the widespread adoption of embalming. After the war, entrepreneurs saw a business opportunity. Embalming schools opened. Funeral "parlors" replaced the family parlor. By the early 20th century, the funeral industry had established itself as the primary intermediary between families and death, and the home funeral — the way humans had cared for their dead for millennia — gradually faded from mainstream American practice.

Today, that trajectory is reversing. The home funeral movement, which began quietly in the 1990s with grassroots advocates and a handful of pioneering organizations, has grown into a recognized and respected alternative. Books, workshops, online communities, and professional home funeral guides have made the knowledge and skills of after-death care accessible to any family that wants them. The movement is not anti-funeral-home. Many families who hold home funerals work cooperatively with funeral directors for specific services like cremation or transportation. Rather, the movement is about restoring choice, agency, and intimacy to a process that had become almost entirely outsourced.

For many families, reclaiming the care of their dead is a way of reclaiming something essential about what it means to be human — the willingness to be present with loss, to offer one's hands in service, and to honor the body that carried a beloved life.

Legal Requirements by State

Home funerals are legal in all 50 U.S. states. However, the specific laws governing who may care for the body, file paperwork, and transport the deceased vary from state to state. Understanding your state's requirements is an essential first step in planning a home funeral. The information below provides a general overview; families should always verify current laws with their state's vital records office or department of health.

States Where Families Can Handle Everything Themselves

The majority of U.S. states allow families to serve as the responsible party for all aspects of after-death care without involving a licensed funeral director. In these states, families may legally:

  • Care for and prepare the body at home
  • File the death certificate with the local registrar
  • Obtain a burial or transit permit
  • Transport the body to a crematory or cemetery
  • Arrange for cremation or burial directly

States known for being especially supportive of family-directed funerals include Colorado, Vermont, Maine, Oregon, Washington, California, Minnesota, and North Carolina, among many others. These states have clear legal frameworks that affirm the family's right to act without a funeral director.

States Requiring Funeral Director Involvement

A smaller number of states require a licensed funeral director to be involved in at least some aspects of the process. The most commonly cited states include:

  • Connecticut — Requires a funeral director to obtain the burial permit
  • Indiana — Requires a funeral director to sign the death certificate and file it
  • Louisiana — Requires a funeral director for most aspects of disposition
  • Nebraska — Requires a funeral director to file the death certificate
  • New York — Requires a funeral director to sign the death certificate

Even in these states, families can still care for the body at home, hold a vigil, and direct the ceremony. The funeral director's involvement may be limited to specific paperwork tasks. Some funeral directors are willing to provide these limited services for a modest fee, allowing the family to handle everything else independently.

Filing Death Certificates

In every state, a death certificate must be filed with the local or state vital records office, typically within 24 to 72 hours of the death. The death certificate requires information from two sources: the attending physician, medical examiner, or coroner (who certifies the cause and manner of death) and the funeral director or responsible party (who provides demographic and disposition information). In states that allow family-directed funerals, a family member can serve as the responsible party and complete their portion of the certificate. Certified copies of the death certificate are needed for closing bank accounts, filing insurance claims, and other administrative matters — fees for copies typically range from $5 to $25 per copy depending on the state.

Transportation and Burial/Transit Permits

Most states require a burial or transit permit before a body can be transported, buried, or cremated. This permit is generally issued by the local registrar or health department after the death certificate has been filed. In states that allow family-directed care, families can obtain this permit themselves. When transporting the body, families should keep the permit with them at all times, as law enforcement may request to see it. Most families transport the body in a personal vehicle — a minivan, SUV, or pickup truck — with the body placed on a flat surface and covered respectfully.

Timeframes for Burial or Cremation

Some states set specific timeframes within which a body must be buried, cremated, or embalmed. These windows typically range from 24 to 72 hours after death, though many states have no hard deadline as long as the body is properly preserved or refrigerated. In practice, most home funerals take place within one to three days, which falls comfortably within these limits. Dry ice and other cooling methods are widely accepted as alternatives to embalming for maintaining the body during this period.

What Is Involved in a Home Funeral

The practical aspects of a home funeral may seem daunting at first, but families who have been through the process consistently describe it as manageable, meaningful, and deeply healing. Here is what is typically involved, step by step.

Caring for the Body at Home: Cooling Methods

The most important practical concern in a home funeral is keeping the body cool to slow the natural process of decomposition. Embalming is not necessary and is not used in home funerals. Instead, families rely on one or more of the following cooling methods:

  • Dry ice: This is the most widely used and effective cooling method for home funerals. Blocks or slabs of dry ice are placed around the torso of the body — particularly along the abdomen and upper chest — and replaced every 18 to 24 hours. Approximately 20 to 30 pounds of dry ice per day is typically sufficient. Dry ice is available from ice companies, some grocery stores, and welding supply shops, and costs roughly $1 to $3 per pound. Important safety note: dry ice must be handled with gloves and used in a well-ventilated room, as it releases carbon dioxide gas as it sublimates.
  • Techni-Ice or reusable gel packs: Reusable cooling packs, particularly the brand Techni-Ice, can be frozen and placed around the body as a supplement to or substitute for dry ice. They are less cold than dry ice but easier to handle and reuse. They are especially useful in the first hours after death while families are sourcing dry ice.
  • Air conditioning: Keeping the room as cool as possible — ideally 60 degrees Fahrenheit or lower — slows decomposition significantly. In cooler climates or during winter months, opening windows may be sufficient. Air conditioning alone is generally not enough for more than 24 hours, but it is an important complement to other cooling methods.
  • Fans and ventilation: Good air circulation helps reduce moisture and keep the area around the body fresh. A small fan directed toward the body (not blowing across it) can help. Keeping the door to the room open also improves air exchange.

With proper cooling, a body can be maintained at home respectfully and without difficulty for one to three days, and sometimes longer in cooler conditions.

Bathing, Dressing, and Preparing the Deceased

Many families find that bathing and dressing their loved one is one of the most tender and meaningful parts of the home funeral experience. It is a final act of physical care — an intimate, unhurried goodbye that no professional service can replicate.

The body can be gently washed with warm water and a soft cloth. Some families use a favorite soap or add essential oils like lavender to the water. Hair can be washed and styled. The person can be dressed in clothing that was meaningful to them — a favorite outfit, a religious garment, a handmade shroud, or even pajamas. Some families apply a light dusting of cornstarch or baking soda to absorb moisture and maintain freshness.

The body is typically placed on a bed, a table, or a cooling board — a flat surface that allows dry ice to be positioned beneath and around the torso. The person can be covered with a favorite blanket or quilt. Fresh flowers, candles, photographs, and meaningful objects are often placed nearby. Many families describe the room as feeling peaceful, sacred, and surprisingly natural.

Legal Paperwork and Notifications

After a death occurs, several administrative steps must be taken regardless of whether the family is using a funeral home or handling the process independently:

  • Pronouncement of death: A physician, nurse, hospice worker, medical examiner, or coroner must officially pronounce the person dead. If the death occurs at home under hospice care, the hospice nurse typically handles this. If the death is unexpected, call 911 or the non-emergency line to have a medical professional respond.
  • Death certificate: The attending physician or medical examiner completes the medical portion. In family-directed states, a family member completes the demographic and disposition sections and files the certificate with the local registrar.
  • Burial or transit permit: Obtained from the local registrar after the death certificate is filed. This permit is required before the body can be transported, buried, or cremated.
  • Notifications: The family should notify Social Security, the person's employer or pension provider, life insurance companies, banks, and other relevant institutions. Our Social Security death benefit guide covers the process and timeline for that particular notification.

Timeframes: Typically One to Three Days at Home

Most home funerals last between one and three days. This allows enough time for family members to gather, for friends and extended family to visit, for a ceremony or vigil to take place, and for the family to process the initial shock of loss in a supported, unhurried environment. Some families keep the body at home for just a few hours; others maintain a vigil for three days or more. The length of the home funeral is a personal decision, guided by the family's emotional needs, cultural and religious practices, and the practical effectiveness of their cooling methods.

Costs of a Home Funeral

One of the most significant advantages of a home funeral is the potential for substantial cost savings. While a traditional funeral arranged through a funeral home typically costs between $7,000 and $15,000 (and often more), a home funeral generally costs between $1,000 and $4,000 in total, depending on the family's choices and location. The savings come from providing the care, preparation, and ceremony yourself, rather than purchasing these as professional services.

Typical Home Funeral Expense Breakdown

  • Dry ice or cooling supplies ($50 – $200): The primary ongoing expense during the home vigil. Twenty to thirty pounds of dry ice per day at $1 to $3 per pound, over one to three days, typically totals $50 to $150. Reusable gel packs are a one-time purchase of $20 to $50.
  • Shroud, simple casket, or container ($100 – $1,500): A burial shroud made of natural fabric costs $100 to $500. A simple pine casket ranges from $400 to $1,500. A cardboard cremation container costs $50 to $200. Some families use a favorite blanket or quilt as a shroud, which costs nothing additional.
  • Death certificate filing fees ($5 – $50 per copy): Most families need multiple certified copies. At $10 to $25 per copy in most states, ordering five to ten copies typically costs $50 to $250 total.
  • Transportation costs ($0 – $500): If the family transports the body themselves using a personal vehicle, the cost is essentially zero beyond fuel. If the family hires a private transportation service or uses a funeral home for transport only, fees typically range from $100 to $500.
  • Home funeral guide or death midwife ($200 – $800): Optional but highly recommended for first-time families. A home funeral guide can help with every aspect of the process. Fees vary widely by practitioner and region.
  • Miscellaneous supplies ($25 – $100): Candles, essential oils, cleaning supplies, cotton batting, or other items the family may want for preparation and vigil.

Cremation or Burial Costs (Separate)

The final disposition of the body — cremation or burial — is a separate cost from the home funeral itself. Families should budget for one of the following:

  • Direct cremation ($1,000 – $3,500): The crematory fee for processing the body after the home vigil is complete. Many crematories will accept the body directly from a family. See our direct cremation cost guide for detailed pricing.
  • Green or natural burial ($1,000 – $4,000): Cemetery plot, opening and closing fees, and any required container. This pairs naturally with a home funeral. See our green burial options guide for details.
  • Traditional burial ($2,000 – $8,000+): Cemetery plot, vault, opening and closing fees, and headstone. Families who hold a home funeral can still choose a conventional burial if they prefer.

Green Burial Connection

Home funerals and green burial are natural companions. Both are rooted in simplicity, environmental stewardship, and the belief that caring for the dead is a fundamental human act, not a commercial transaction. Many families who choose a home funeral also choose green burial as the final disposition method, creating a seamless and coherent experience from death through burial.

In a home funeral followed by green burial, the body is never embalmed, never placed in a sealed metal casket, and never enclosed in a concrete vault. The person is cared for by their family, wrapped in a natural shroud or placed in a biodegradable container, and returned to the earth in a natural setting. The simplicity of this approach is deeply appealing to families who want to minimize their environmental footprint and honor the natural cycle of life and death.

Green cemeteries and natural burial grounds are generally very receptive to families who have held a home funeral. Many green cemeteries allow families to participate directly in the burial — helping to carry the body, lowering it into the grave, and placing the first shovelfuls of earth. This participatory approach extends the hands-on, family-directed nature of the home funeral through the very last moment.

For families interested in exploring this connection further, our comprehensive green burial options guide covers everything from finding a green cemetery to understanding container requirements and costs.

Finding Support

While many families successfully manage every aspect of a home funeral independently, support is available and can make the experience smoother, especially for families who have not done this before. A range of professionals and organizations exist to help families navigate the practical, legal, and emotional dimensions of home-based after-death care.

Home Funeral Guides and Death Midwives

A home funeral guide, also known as a death midwife or end-of-life doula, is a trained professional who helps families through the after-death care process. They are not licensed funeral directors, but they bring deep knowledge and practical experience to the work. A home funeral guide can:

  • Explain your state's legal requirements and help you navigate the paperwork
  • Teach you how to cool, bathe, and dress the body
  • Help you set up a room for the vigil
  • Guide you through the emotional aspects of hands-on care
  • Coordinate with crematories, cemeteries, or funeral homes for specific services
  • Assist with death certificate filing and permit acquisition

Fees for home funeral guides vary widely, typically ranging from $200 to $800 depending on the level of involvement and the practitioner's experience. Some guides offer sliding-scale fees for families with limited resources.

National Home Funeral Alliance (NHFA)

The National Home Funeral Alliance is the leading advocacy and education organization for home funerals in the United States. The NHFA maintains a directory of home funeral guides, offers educational resources and workshops, and advocates for families' rights to care for their own dead. Their website is an essential starting point for any family considering a home funeral.

National End-of-Life Doula Alliance (NEDA)

The National End-of-Life Doula Alliance supports doulas who work with dying individuals and their families, both before and after death. Many end-of-life doulas are trained in home funeral care and can provide support that spans the entire transition from dying to disposition.

Funeral Consumers Alliance

The Funeral Consumers Alliance is a nonprofit organization that educates consumers about their rights and options in funeral planning. While not specifically focused on home funerals, the FCA provides valuable information about state laws, consumer protections, and affordable funeral alternatives. They have local affiliates in many states. For more on your consumer rights, see our FTC Funeral Rule guide.

Sympathetic Funeral Directors

Some families find it helpful to work with a funeral director who supports home funerals. A sympathetic funeral director can provide specific services — such as filing the death certificate in states that require it, arranging cremation, or providing transportation — while allowing the family to handle everything else. Many funeral directors are open to this collaborative approach, especially if the family communicates their wishes clearly. Ask upfront whether the funeral director has experience supporting home funerals and request an itemized price list for only the services you need.

Is a Home Funeral Right for Your Family?

A home funeral is not for every family, and there is absolutely no judgment in choosing a different path. The decision to care for a loved one at home after death is deeply personal and depends on a range of factors. Here are some considerations that may help you think through whether a home funeral feels right for your family.

Emotional Readiness

Caring for the body of someone you love requires emotional willingness. For some people, being physically present with the body and providing hands-on care brings comfort, closure, and a sense of completion. For others, the idea is too overwhelming during an already painful time. Both responses are valid. It is worth having an honest conversation with the people who would be most involved, and respecting everyone's boundaries. Some families find that their initial apprehension gives way to a deep sense of peace once the process begins. Others know from the start that they would prefer professional support.

Physical Space

A home funeral requires a room where the body can be laid out and visitors can gather. The room should be cool and well-ventilated, with enough space for the body on a bed or table, seating for visitors, and room for cooling supplies. A bedroom, living room, or enclosed porch can all work well. Families who live in very small apartments or lack a suitable space may need to consider alternatives. Some families hold the vigil in a community center, a place of worship, or a friend's home instead.

Family Dynamics

Home funerals work best when the family members involved are in general agreement about the approach. If there is significant disagreement — for example, if some family members are distressed by the idea of the body remaining at home — it is important to listen and find a path that honors everyone's feelings. Sometimes a compromise is possible: the family might hold the body at home for a shorter period, or arrange for certain family members to participate in specific parts of the process while others take a different role.

Cultural and Religious Compatibility

Many cultural and religious traditions are deeply compatible with home funerals. Jewish tahara (ritual washing), Muslim ghusl (washing of the body), and many Indigenous and African American traditions of family-directed care all share common ground with the home funeral ethos. Buddhist and Hindu families may also find that home-based vigils align with their spiritual practices. Some Christian traditions, particularly among communities that have maintained rural or Appalachian customs, have never fully stopped practicing home funerals. If your cultural or religious tradition includes specific requirements for after-death care, a home funeral may allow you to fulfill those practices more authentically than a conventional funeral home setting.

Practical Considerations

Families should also consider the cause of death. If the death involves circumstances that require investigation by a medical examiner or coroner — such as an unexpected death, an accident, or a death not attended by a physician — the body may need to be taken for examination before a home funeral can begin. In such cases, the body can usually be returned to the family afterward. Deaths from certain infectious diseases may also require specific handling protocols. If you are uncertain about any medical or legal considerations, consult with your state's vital records office or a home funeral guide.

You Can Choose Pieces, Not the Whole

It is important to remember that a home funeral does not have to be all or nothing. Some families keep the body at home for a few hours of private time before transferring care to a funeral home. Others handle the vigil and ceremony at home but rely on a funeral director for paperwork and transportation. The degree of involvement is entirely up to you. There is no wrong way to do this, as long as the choices you make come from a place of love and respect.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to have a home funeral?

Home funerals are legal in all 50 U.S. states. However, the degree to which families can handle arrangements independently varies. Most states allow families to care for the body, file paperwork, and transport the deceased without a funeral director. A smaller number of states — including Connecticut, Indiana, Louisiana, Nebraska, and New York — require a licensed funeral director to be involved in certain steps such as filing the death certificate or obtaining a burial/transit permit. Families should check their specific state's regulations before planning.

How much does a home funeral cost?

A home funeral typically costs between $1,000 and $4,000 in total, compared to $7,000 to $15,000 for a traditional funeral arranged through a funeral home. The primary expenses include dry ice or cooling supplies ($50–$200), a shroud or simple container ($100–$1,500), death certificate filing fees ($5–$50 per copy), and transportation if needed ($100–$500). Cremation or burial costs are separate and vary by location and method. The significant savings come from families providing the care, preparation, and ceremony themselves.

How long can you keep a body at home after death?

In most states, there is no specific legal time limit for how long a body may remain at home, as long as it is properly preserved or cooled. Most families who hold a home funeral keep the body at home for one to three days, which allows time for family and friends to visit, participate in caring for the person, and hold a ceremony or vigil. With proper cooling using dry ice, Techni-Ice packs, or air conditioning, the body can be maintained respectfully for this period. Some states do require burial or cremation within a specific timeframe — often 24 to 72 hours — if the body has not been embalmed or refrigerated.

Do you need to embalm a body for a home funeral?

No. Embalming is not required for a home funeral in any state. No state law mandates embalming for all deaths. Instead, the body is kept cool using dry ice, Techni-Ice reusable packs, or air conditioning set to a low temperature. This approach is both legal and effective for the typical one-to-three-day period of a home funeral. Embalming is a choice, not a necessity, and many families find that natural cooling methods are simpler, less invasive, and more aligned with the intimate nature of home-based care.

What is a death midwife or end-of-life doula?

A death midwife (also called a home funeral guide or end-of-life doula) is a trained professional who supports families through the dying process and after-death care. They can help families understand legal requirements, prepare the body, obtain necessary documents, coordinate with cremation facilities or cemeteries, and navigate the emotional aspects of caring for a loved one at home. Death midwives are not licensed funeral directors, but they serve as knowledgeable companions who empower families to direct the process themselves. The National Home Funeral Alliance and the National End-of-Life Doula Alliance maintain directories of trained practitioners.

Can you transport a body yourself without a funeral home?

In most states, families have the legal right to transport the body of a deceased loved one without involving a funeral home. This applies to transporting the body from the place of death to the family home, and from the home to a cemetery or crematory. A few states require a funeral director to handle or authorize transportation, so it is important to verify your state's specific rules. When families do transport the body themselves, they typically use a personal vehicle with the body placed on a flat surface in the back. A burial/transit permit or other documentation is generally required during transport.
