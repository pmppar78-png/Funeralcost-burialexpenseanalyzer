Payment ideas families often explore

Many families are surprised by how quickly funeral and burial costs add up. The goal of this section is to help you understand common paths people research — not to tell you what to do or whether you qualify for any option.

  • Existing life insurance policies and whether they can be assigned to the funeral home.
  • Payable-on-death bank accounts or small savings earmarked for final expenses.
  • Short-term payment plans with funeral homes or third-party financing companies.
  • Veterans benefits, union benefits, or employer-sponsored assistance where available.
  • State or county assistance for indigent burials or hardship situations.
  • Community support such as faith communities, mutual aid, or fundraising among friends and family.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a funeral cost on average in the United States?

According to the National Funeral Directors Association, the median cost of a funeral with viewing and burial is approximately $7,848, while a funeral with cremation averages around $6,971. However, costs vary widely by state, provider, and the services chosen. Some families spend under $2,000 with direct cremation, while others spend $15,000 or more for elaborate services. The best way to understand costs in your area is to request itemized price lists from several local funeral homes — they are required by federal law to provide them.

What is the difference between a traditional funeral and direct cremation?

A traditional funeral typically includes embalming, a viewing or visitation, a formal ceremony (often at a funeral home chapel or house of worship), and burial in a casket. Direct cremation skips the viewing and formal ceremony entirely — the body is cremated shortly after death, and the family may hold a memorial service later if they wish. Direct cremation is usually the least expensive option and is growing in popularity across the country.

Am I required to buy a casket from the funeral home?

No. Under the FTC Funeral Rule, funeral homes must accept caskets purchased from third-party retailers (including online sellers) without charging a handling fee. This can sometimes save families hundreds or thousands of dollars. It's worth comparing prices before making a decision.

What is the FTC Funeral Rule?

The FTC Funeral Rule is a federal regulation enforced by the Federal Trade Commission. It requires funeral providers to give you itemized price lists (called a General Price List, or GPL) when you ask. It allows you to choose only the goods and services you want, without being forced into package deals. It also prohibits funeral homes from making false claims about legal requirements — for example, they cannot tell you that embalming is required by law when it isn't (in most states). If you believe a funeral home has violated the Funeral Rule, you can file a complaint with the FTC.

Is embalming legally required?

In most states, embalming is not required by law. It is typically optional, though funeral homes may require it for certain services such as an open-casket viewing. Alternatives include refrigeration or dry ice. If you're told embalming is mandatory, ask for the specific law or regulation — in most cases, it is a funeral home policy, not a legal requirement.

Can I plan a funeral without a funeral home?

In many states, families have the legal right to handle some or all funeral arrangements themselves, including transporting the body, preparing it for burial or cremation, and holding a ceremony. This is sometimes called a "home funeral" or "family-directed funeral." However, the laws vary by state, and some states require a licensed funeral director for certain steps. Research your state's laws or consult your state's funeral board before making arrangements independently.

Glossary of common funeral terms

Funeral planning involves terminology that many families are hearing for the first time during an already difficult period. This glossary explains some of the most common terms in plain language.

General Price List (GPL)
A printed list of all goods and services offered by a funeral home, along with their prices. Funeral homes are required by federal law to give you this list when you ask.
Embalming
A chemical process used to temporarily preserve the body for viewing or transport. Not legally required in most states and is typically optional.
Direct cremation
Cremation that takes place shortly after death without a viewing, visitation, or ceremony beforehand. Usually the least expensive option.
Direct burial
Burial that happens shortly after death, skipping embalming, viewing, and formal ceremony. A simple casket or shroud is typically used.
Casket vs. coffin
A casket is rectangular with a split lid; a coffin is wider at the shoulders and tapers at the feet. In the U.S., "casket" is the more commonly used term.
Vault or grave liner
A concrete or metal container placed in the ground before the casket is lowered. Many cemeteries require one, but it is not a universal legal requirement.
Preneed contract
An agreement to pre-plan and sometimes pre-pay for funeral services before death occurs. Consumer protections vary widely by state, so read carefully.
Opening and closing fees
Charges from the cemetery for digging the grave (opening) and filling it in (closing) after burial. These fees are separate from the cost of the burial plot.

Average funeral and burial expenses vary widely by state and even by county. Some states have strong consumer protection rules, price disclosure requirements, or public guides that outline typical fees.
