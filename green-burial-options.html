Green Burial Options: Natural, Eco-Friendly & Affordable Alternatives

If you are considering a green burial for yourself or someone you love, you are exploring one of the most meaningful, gentle, and increasingly popular ways to honor a life. Green burial is not a trend or a novelty — it is a return to the way humans have cared for their dead for thousands of years, before the modern funeral industry introduced embalming chemicals, metal caskets, and concrete vaults. This guide is here to walk you through every aspect of green burial with honesty, compassion, and practical detail, so you can make decisions that feel right for your family, your values, and your budget. Whatever path you choose, it is the right one if it comes from a place of love.

What Is Green Burial?

Green burial, sometimes called natural burial, is a way of caring for the dead that prioritizes simplicity, environmental responsibility, and the natural return of the body to the earth. In a green burial, the body is not embalmed with chemical preservatives. It is placed in a biodegradable container — a simple wooden casket, a wicker basket, a cotton or linen shroud — and buried directly in the ground without a concrete vault or liner.

The philosophy behind green burial is rooted in the understanding that death is part of the natural cycle of life. Rather than attempting to slow decomposition through chemicals and sealed containers, green burial allows the body to nourish the earth, return its nutrients to the soil, and contribute to the growth of new life. For many families, there is a deep and abiding comfort in this idea — that their loved one becomes part of the living landscape, woven into the roots of trees and the blossoms of wildflowers.

The green burial movement has grown steadily over the past two decades. According to the National Funeral Directors Association, more than 60 percent of Americans now express interest in environmentally friendly funeral options. The Green Burial Council, the leading certification organization for green burial providers, reports that the number of certified green cemeteries and funeral homes continues to increase each year. This is not a fringe choice. It is a growing, mainstream option embraced by families of every background, faith, and financial situation.

Green Burial Costs: What to Expect

One of the most encouraging aspects of green burial is that it is typically far less expensive than a traditional burial. While a conventional funeral with burial averages $7,000 to $12,000 or more when you factor in the casket, vault, embalming, and cemetery fees, a green burial generally costs between $1,000 and $4,000 in total.

The savings come from eliminating several of the most expensive components of traditional burial:

  • No embalming ($0 saved vs. $500 – $1,000): Green burial skips chemical embalming entirely. The body may be refrigerated or kept cool with dry ice instead.
  • Biodegradable container ($200 – $1,500 vs. $2,500 – $10,000): Instead of a polished metal or hardwood casket, families choose a simple pine box, wicker casket, or burial shroud.
  • No vault or liner ($0 saved vs. $1,000 – $10,000): Green cemeteries do not require concrete vaults. The body is placed directly in the earth.
  • Cemetery plot at a natural burial ground ($500 – $4,000): Plot costs at green cemeteries vary by location but are often comparable to or less than conventional cemeteries.
  • Opening and closing the grave ($300 – $1,000): Some natural burial grounds allow family members to help dig and fill the grave, which can reduce or eliminate this fee.

Types of Green Burial

Green burial is not a single, rigid practice. It exists on a spectrum, and families can choose the approach that best fits their values, circumstances, and comfort level.

Conservation Burial

Conservation burial represents the gold standard of green burial. In a conservation cemetery, the burial ground is permanently protected by a legal conservation easement, which means the land can never be sold, developed, or repurposed. Burial fees directly fund the acquisition and restoration of natural habitat — native prairies, forests, wetlands, or meadows. Conservation cemeteries are certified by the Green Burial Council and must meet rigorous environmental standards. For families who want their loved one's resting place to actively contribute to the preservation of the natural world, conservation burial is the most impactful choice.

Natural Burial

Natural burial grounds operate with a commitment to environmental sustainability but may not carry the legal protections of a conservation easement. These cemeteries prohibit embalming, require biodegradable containers, and do not allow conventional headstones. Graves are typically marked with native plants, flat field stones, or GPS coordinates. Natural burial grounds often feel more like parks or nature preserves than traditional cemeteries, offering a peaceful and restorative setting for families who visit.

Hybrid Green Burial

Many conventional cemeteries now offer designated green burial sections within their existing grounds. These hybrid options allow families to choose green burial without traveling to a dedicated natural cemetery. The green section may waive vault requirements and permit biodegradable containers, while the rest of the cemetery continues to operate traditionally. This is a practical option for families who want green burial in a convenient location or near other family members already interred at the cemetery.

Home Burial

In many states, families have the legal right to bury a loved one on private property. Home burial is inherently green when done without embalming and with a biodegradable container. It offers the most personal and intimate experience — the person rests on land that holds meaning for the family. However, home burial requires careful attention to local zoning laws, minimum acreage requirements, setback distances from wells and water sources, and deed recording requirements. Not all states permit home burial without the involvement of a licensed funeral director, so research your state's regulations carefully.

Green Burial Containers and Shrouds

The container used in a green burial must be fully biodegradable, allowing the body and the container to decompose naturally and return to the earth. The range of options has expanded significantly in recent years, giving families choices that are both beautiful and meaningful.

  • Pine or poplar caskets ($400 – $1,500): Simple, unfinished wood caskets are the most common choice for green burial. They are sturdy enough to transport the body and dignified in their simplicity. Some families build the casket themselves as a labor of love, or purchase one from a local woodworker.
  • Wicker and bamboo caskets ($600 – $2,000): Handwoven wicker, willow, and bamboo caskets are lightweight, elegant, and completely biodegradable. They have a warm, natural appearance that many families find comforting.
  • Seagrass and banana leaf caskets ($500 – $1,500): These handcrafted caskets are woven from sustainably harvested natural fibers. They are fair-trade products often made by artisan cooperatives in developing countries.
  • Burial shrouds ($100 – $500): A shroud is a simple cloth wrapping, typically made from unbleached cotton, linen, wool, or silk. Shroud burial is one of the oldest and most universal forms of body preparation, practiced across cultures and centuries. Many shroud designs include a carrying board to make transport easier.
  • Mushroom and mycelium suits ($1,000 – $1,500): A newer innovation, these burial garments are infused with mushroom spores and other microorganisms that are designed to accelerate decomposition, neutralize toxins, and deliver nutrients to surrounding plant life. While the science is still emerging, many families find the concept deeply appealing.
  • Cardboard caskets ($50 – $200): The most affordable option, cardboard caskets are plain but entirely functional. Some families personalize them by writing messages, drawing pictures, or attaching photographs to the outside — transforming a humble container into a deeply personal tribute.

Under the FTC Funeral Rule, funeral homes are required to accept any casket or container you provide, and they cannot charge you a handling fee for using one purchased elsewhere. This means you are free to buy a green burial container from an independent retailer, online seller, or craftsperson. Finding a Green Cemetery

The number of green cemeteries in the United States is growing, but they are not yet available in every community. Finding the right one requires a bit of research, and understanding the certification system can help you evaluate your options.

Green Burial Council Certification

The Green Burial Council (GBC) is the leading independent certification organization for green burial providers. The GBC certifies cemeteries, funeral homes, and burial products at different levels:

  • Hybrid cemeteries — conventional cemeteries that offer a green burial option within a designated section
  • Natural burial grounds — cemeteries entirely dedicated to green burial practices
  • Conservation cemeteries — the highest standard, with legally protected land and active ecological restoration

Families can search the GBC directory at greenburialcouncil.org to find certified providers in their area. Certification gives families confidence that the cemetery meets genuine environmental standards and is not simply using "green" as a marketing label.

Questions to Ask a Green Cemetery

When evaluating a green cemetery, consider asking:

  • Is the cemetery certified by the Green Burial Council, and at what level?
  • What types of containers are permitted?
  • Are families allowed to participate in the burial (helping to lower the body, filling the grave)?
  • How are graves marked, and can family members visit easily?
  • What is the total cost, including plot, opening and closing, and any administrative fees?
  • Is the land protected by a conservation easement?
  • What happens to the cemetery if the operating organization changes?

Green Burial and Religion

Green burial is compatible with many of the world's major faith traditions, and in some cases, it aligns more closely with religious teachings than modern conventional burial does.

  • Judaism: Jewish burial law (halacha) has long called for simple, natural burial. The body is washed in a ritual called tahara, wrapped in a plain linen shroud (tachrichim), and placed in a simple wooden casket without metal hardware. Embalming is forbidden. Green burial is deeply consistent with Jewish tradition.
  • Islam: Islamic burial practice requires that the body be washed, wrapped in a white cotton shroud (kafan), and buried as soon as possible without a casket or with a simple one. Embalming and cremation are generally prohibited. Green burial aligns naturally with these practices.
  • Christianity: Most Christian denominations do not prescribe specific burial methods, and many welcome green burial as an expression of stewardship over God's creation. The Catholic Church permits green burial as long as the body is treated with reverence. Protestant and Orthodox traditions generally leave the choice to the family.
  • Buddhism: Buddhist teachings on impermanence and the interconnectedness of all life resonate with the philosophy of green burial. The body's natural return to the earth can be seen as a reflection of the cycle of existence.
  • Hinduism: While cremation is the traditional Hindu practice, some Hindus choose green burial as an alternative that honors the principle of returning the body to the five elements of nature.
  • Secular and non-religious: For families without a specific faith tradition, green burial offers a way to create meaning through connection to the natural world, environmental stewardship, and the comforting idea that life continues in new forms.

Environmental Impact

Understanding the environmental footprint of different burial and cremation methods can help families make choices that align with their values. The data is clear: green burial has a significantly smaller impact on the earth than conventional alternatives.

Traditional Burial: The Environmental Cost

Every year in the United States, conventional burials use an estimated 4.3 million gallons of embalming fluid (which contains formaldehyde, a known carcinogen), 20 million board feet of hardwood for caskets, 1.6 million tons of concrete for vaults, and 17,000 tons of copper and bronze for caskets. These materials are buried in the ground where they do not biodegrade, and the chemicals can leach into soil and groundwater over time.

Cremation: A Smaller but Real Footprint

Cremation uses less land and fewer materials, but it is not without environmental impact. A single cremation requires the energy equivalent of roughly 28 gallons of fuel and releases approximately 400 to 600 pounds of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, along with trace amounts of mercury from dental fillings. The growing popularity of cremation has reduced some of the environmental burdens of burial, but it is not a zero-impact choice.

Green Burial: Returning to the Earth

Green burial eliminates virtually all of the industrial materials and chemicals involved in conventional burial. No formaldehyde. No concrete. No metal. The body decomposes naturally, its nutrients feeding the microorganisms, plants, and trees in the surrounding soil. In conservation cemeteries, each burial actively contributes to the protection and restoration of natural habitat. Over time, the cemetery becomes a thriving ecosystem — a forest, a meadow, a wetland — that exists because families chose to honor their loved ones in this way.

State Regulations and Legal Considerations

Green burial is legal in every U.S. state, but the specific regulations surrounding burial practices vary from state to state. Understanding these rules can help you plan with confidence and avoid unnecessary complications.

Embalming Requirements

No state requires embalming for all burials. Some states require embalming only in specific circumstances, such as when the body will be transported across state lines by common carrier, or when there is a lengthy delay between death and burial. In all other cases, refrigeration or dry ice is a legal and accepted alternative. If a funeral home tells you that embalming is "required," ask them to cite the specific state statute. In most cases, it is a recommendation or a funeral home policy, not a law.

Vault and Container Requirements

No state law requires a burial vault or outer container. However, many individual cemeteries require them to prevent the ground from settling over time, which makes mowing and maintenance easier. Green cemeteries and green burial sections within conventional cemeteries waive this requirement. If you want a green burial at a conventional cemetery, ask specifically about their vault policy and whether exceptions can be made.

Home Burial on Private Land

Most states allow burial on private land, but requirements vary. Some states have minimum acreage requirements. Most require that the burial site be a certain distance from wells, water sources, and property lines. Some require recording the burial on the property deed. A few states require the involvement of a licensed funeral director. Always check with your county or municipality before planning a home burial.

FTC Funeral Rule Protections

The Federal Trade Commission's Funeral Rule protects all consumers, including those planning green burials. Funeral homes must provide an itemized price list upon request, allow you to purchase only the services you want, and accept caskets or containers purchased from third-party sources without charging a handling fee. These protections ensure that you are not pressured into purchasing unnecessary services or products. How to Plan a Green Burial

Planning a green burial is straightforward, but it does require some advance preparation — especially because green cemeteries are not yet available in every community. Whether you are planning ahead for yourself or making arrangements after a loss, these steps will help guide you through the process.

  1. Research green cemeteries in your area. Start with the Green Burial Council directory at greenburialcouncil.org. Also call local cemeteries to ask if they offer a green burial section. If no green cemetery is nearby, consider whether a short distance is worth traveling, or whether a hybrid option at a local conventional cemetery might work.
  2. Contact the cemetery and ask detailed questions. Request a complete price list. Ask about container requirements, grave marking options, the availability of specific plots, and whether families can participate in the burial process. Clarify any policies about visitation and long-term access to the site.
  3. Choose a funeral home that supports green burial. Not all funeral homes are experienced with green burial, so ask directly. A supportive funeral home will be familiar with biodegradable containers, skip embalming without resistance, and coordinate smoothly with the green cemetery. The Green Burial Council also certifies funeral homes.
  4. Select a burial container or shroud. Decide whether you want a simple wooden casket, a wicker basket, a shroud, or another biodegradable option. You can purchase these online, from the funeral home, or from specialty retailers. Remember that under the FTC Funeral Rule, the funeral home must accept any container you provide.
  5. Discuss your wishes with your family. Green burial may be unfamiliar to some family members. Sharing your reasons and the practical details can help everyone feel comfortable and supported. If you are planning ahead, put your wishes in writing and let your loved ones know where to find the document.
  6. Consider pre-planning and pre-payment. Many green cemeteries accept advance arrangements and pre-payment for plots. Pre-planning ensures that your wishes are documented and reduces the burden on your family during a difficult time. Be sure to understand the refund and transfer policies before committing funds.
  7. Plan the ceremony. A green burial can include any type of service that is meaningful to your family — a graveside ceremony, a celebration of life at home, a religious service, or a quiet gathering in nature. The ceremony is entirely separate from the burial method, so you have complete freedom to design a farewell that reflects the life being honored.

Combining Green Burial with a Meaningful Service

One of the most common misconceptions about green burial is that it means giving up the rituals, ceremonies, and traditions that bring comfort during grief. Nothing could be further from the truth. Green burial is simply a method of disposition — what you do with the body. The ceremony, the gathering, the stories, the music, the prayers — all of that is entirely up to you and your family.

Graveside Ceremonies

Many families hold a graveside ceremony at the natural burial ground itself. These services are often described as among the most moving and healing experiences families have shared. The natural setting — open sky, birdsong, the scent of earth and wildflowers — creates an atmosphere that no funeral home chapel can replicate. Families may read poems, share memories, sing hymns, or simply stand together in silence. Some families choose to help lower the body into the grave and place the first shovelfuls of earth, an ancient act of love that provides a powerful sense of closure.

Celebrations of Life

A celebration of life can be held before or after the burial, at any location that feels meaningful. A backyard gathering, a community hall, a favorite restaurant, a public park — the setting should reflect the personality and passions of the person being remembered. Potluck meals, photo displays, favorite music playlists, and open invitations to share stories all create an atmosphere of warmth and genuine connection.

Memorial Plantings and Living Tributes

In many green cemeteries, families can plant native trees, shrubs, or wildflowers at the gravesite instead of placing a conventional headstone. This creates a living memorial that grows and changes with the seasons, providing a place of beauty and renewal that family members can visit for years to come. Some families also plant memorial gardens at home, dedicate trees in public parks, or contribute to reforestation projects in their loved one's name.

Religious and Cultural Rituals

Green burial can be seamlessly integrated with religious services, cultural ceremonies, and family traditions. A rabbi can lead prayers at a green graveside. A priest can offer a blessing over a shroud-wrapped body. A family can hold a traditional wake the night before a green burial. The method of burial does not dictate the ceremony — your faith, your culture, and your family's traditions remain entirely your own.

Lasting Memorials Beyond the Grave

Because green cemeteries often do not permit conventional headstones, families sometimes create alternative memorials: a bench in a favorite park, a scholarship in the person's name, a donation to a cause they cared about, or a handmade memory book shared among family members. These memorials carry forward the spirit of the person in ways that feel alive and purposeful.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a green burial cost?

Green burial typically costs between $1,000 and $4,000, which is significantly less than a traditional burial averaging $7,000 to $12,000. The savings come from eliminating embalming, using a biodegradable container instead of a metal casket, and forgoing a concrete vault. Cemetery plot costs at natural burial grounds generally range from $500 to $4,000 depending on location and whether the land is part of a conservation easement.

Is green burial legal in all states?

Green burial is legal in all 50 U.S. states. No state requires embalming for a standard burial, and no state mandates the use of a vault or non-biodegradable casket in all cases. However, individual cemeteries may have their own rules about containers and burial methods. Some states have specific regulations around home burial on private land, so families should check local ordinances before planning.

Do you need a casket for green burial?

No, a traditional casket is not required for green burial. Most green cemeteries accept biodegradable caskets made of pine, bamboo, wicker, or seagrass, as well as simple burial shrouds. Some families choose a plain wooden box or even a cardboard container. The key requirement is that whatever container is used must be fully biodegradable so that the body can return to the earth naturally.

Can you be embalmed for a green burial?

Traditional embalming with formaldehyde-based chemicals is not compatible with green burial, because these chemicals are toxic to soil and groundwater. However, some providers offer non-toxic, plant-based embalming alternatives that are accepted at certain green cemeteries. In most green burials, the body is simply refrigerated or kept cool with dry ice until the burial takes place, which is both legal and respectful.

What is a conservation cemetery?

A conservation cemetery is a burial ground that is permanently protected through a conservation easement, meaning the land can never be developed. Burial fees help fund the acquisition, restoration, and stewardship of natural habitat. Conservation cemeteries are certified by the Green Burial Council and represent the highest standard of green burial. They protect native ecosystems while giving families a meaningful and lasting way to honor a loved one.

Are green burials religious?

Green burial is not tied to any specific religion, but it is naturally compatible with many faith traditions. Judaism and Islam have long practiced simple, natural burial without embalming or elaborate caskets. Many Christian denominations embrace the idea of returning the body to the earth. Buddhist and Hindu traditions also align with the simplicity and ecological awareness of green burial. Families of any faith or no faith can choose green burial.

How do families find green burial providers?

The Green Burial Council maintains a searchable directory of certified green burial providers, cemeteries, and funeral homes at greenburialcouncil.org. Families can also contact local funeral homes and ask whether they offer green burial options. The Funeral Consumers Alliance and state funeral consumer organizations can provide referrals as well. Many conventional cemeteries now designate green burial sections even if they are not entirely natural burial grounds.

What happens to the body in green burial?

In green burial, the body is placed directly in the earth in a biodegradable container or shroud, without embalming chemicals or a sealed vault. Over time, the body decomposes naturally through the same biological processes that recycle all organic matter. The nutrients return to the soil, nourishing the plants and trees growing above. Most green cemeteries plant native vegetation or wildflowers over burial sites, creating a living memorial.

Can veterans have a green burial?

Yes. Veterans can absolutely have a green burial. While VA national cemeteries have specific requirements that may not align with all green burial practices, veterans can choose green burial at any private natural cemetery and still receive applicable VA burial benefits, including the burial allowance and a government-furnished headstone or marker. Some state veteran cemeteries are also beginning to offer green burial sections. For more on veteran benefits, see our veteran burial benefits guide.

Is green burial cheaper than cremation?

Green burial and direct cremation are often comparable in price. Direct cremation typically costs $1,000 to $3,500, while a simple green burial generally ranges from $1,000 to $4,000. Green burial may cost slightly more when cemetery plot fees are included, but it avoids the energy use and carbon emissions associated with cremation. For families who value both affordability and environmental stewardship, green burial offers a compelling alternative.
